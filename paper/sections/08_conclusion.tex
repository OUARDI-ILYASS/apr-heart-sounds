\section{Conclusion}

This project implemented a heart sound classification pipeline on the public
portion of PhysioNet/CinC 2016 and compared three feature representations across
three classifier families under a single recording-level protocol. The best
configuration, an RBF SVM on MFCC statistics, reaches a modified accuracy of
\num{0.866} on our held-out split, and the log-Mel CNN is statistically
indistinguishable from it on that metric while making significantly fewer total
recording-level errors.

The more useful findings are the ones that came from testing those numbers
rather than reporting them. $k$-means on the unsupervised feature spaces
recovers the recording site better than the diagnosis; per-site modified
accuracy spans \num{0.509}, from near-perfect on the largest sub-database to
below chance level on two others; and the CNN's attribution, though verifiably
dependent on learned weights and mildly enriched in systole
($E_\text{systole} = \num{1.056}$), is not more systole-enriched on correct
predictions than on incorrect ones. Two of the three expectations we set out
with were contradicted and one was only weakly supported.

We therefore conclude that on this corpus the aggregate metric overstates what
the models have learned, and that the diagnostics --- not the leaderboard
position --- are where the informative content of such a study lies. The natural
continuation is to make site an explicit variable: predict the sub-database
directly from the features to quantify the shortcut, and evaluate under
leave-one-site-out rather than stratified splitting, which measures the
generalisation that a screening application would actually require.
