\section{Results III: Explainability as a Test}
\label{sec:xai}

The standard practice of displaying a few heat maps and asserting that a model
``focuses on clinically relevant regions'' is unfalsifiable as stated. This
section states a prediction in advance and then tests it. We use SHAP for the
classical models --- the exact tree estimator for random forests, the
model-agnostic kernel estimator for the SVM~\cite{lundberg2017unified,lundberg2020local}
--- and Grad-CAM at the last convolutional block for the
CNN~\cite{selvaraju2017grad}.

\subsection{Sanity checks first}

Attribution maps mean nothing until they are shown to depend on the trained
weights~\cite{adebayo2018sanity}, so the checks are reported before the
findings. Recomputing Grad-CAM from a randomly re-initialised copy of the
network gives maps that correlate with the trained model's at only \num{0.051}
on average over 100 windows, so the maps reflect learned structure rather than
architecture. Correlating the temporal attribution profile with the signal
envelope gives \num{0.068} over 499 windows, with no window above \num{0.5}:
attribution is not simply tracking loudness, which matters because S1 and S2 are
the loud events and a loudness-driven map would confound any phase-specific
claim. Both checks pass.

A third check is less reassuring. Grad-CAM and Grad-CAM++~\cite{chattopadhay2018grad}
maps correlate at only \num{0.392}. Our temporal conclusions are therefore
specific to the Grad-CAM configuration and should not be assumed to hold for
other attribution variants.

\subsection{Where in frequency?}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_frequency_attribution}
\caption{SHAP attribution mapped to frequency for the four classical
feature/model pairings. PWP features map onto bands exactly; the MFCC profile is
obtained by redistributing attribution through the magnitude of the DCT basis
and is approximate.}
\label{fig:freqattr}
\end{figure}

PWP features belong to exactly one frequency band by construction, so their
attribution maps onto frequency exactly. MFCC features do not: a cepstral
coefficient is a weighted sum over all mel bands, so attribution must be
redistributed through the magnitude of the DCT basis. That projection is
sign-agnostic and therefore approximate, and where the two disagree the PWP
result is the trustworthy one.

All four classical pairings peak at the low edge of the band,
\SI{25}{\hertz}, and Fig.~\ref{fig:freqattr} shows the profiles. Cross-domain
agreement is weak, however: the mean pairwise correlation between the four
profiles is \num{0.514}, and this average conceals a wide range. The two MFCC
models agree almost perfectly with each other (\num{0.961}), but RF--PWP and
SVM--MFCC agree at only \num{0.280}. The high number is largely a statement
about the shared representation rather than about a shared physiological
finding: models built on the same features attribute similarly, models built on
different features do not. We therefore state agreement across feature domains
as weak.

Individually, the leading PWP features are interpretable. The top two by mean
absolute SHAP value are the Shannon entropy of band 0 (25--\SI{51}{\hertz},
\SI{9.5}{\percent} of total attribution) and of band 9
(291--\SI{326}{\hertz}, \SI{7.9}{\percent}), followed by relative energy in
77--\SI{105}{\hertz}. Spectral entropy in a low band and in a high band is a
plausible murmur descriptor --- murmurs are noise-like where S1 and S2 are
tonal. But sign consistency is only \num{0.57} and \num{0.55} respectively,
meaning these features push toward ``abnormal'' barely more often than toward
``normal''. On that basis we describe them as the features the model relies on
most, not as evidence of a murmur detector. For the MFCC models the leading
features are $\mathrm{MFCC}_3$ mean and $\mathrm{MFCC}_0$ statistics; the
prominence of the zeroth coefficient and its derivative, which describe overall
energy and its variation, is not encouraging given Section~\ref{sec:persite}.

\subsection{Where in the cardiac cycle?}

\input{tables/table_alignment}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_alignment_cnn}
\caption{Distribution of per-window systolic enrichment $E_\text{systole}$ for
the CNN. The dashed line at $E=1$ is what uniform temporal attention produces.
The distribution is shifted slightly right of it.}
\label{fig:alignment}
\end{figure}

Each window is labelled into S1, systole, S2 and diastole by an envelope-based
segmenter that operates independently of the model. Given a unit-mass
attribution map, the enrichment for state $s$ is
\begin{equation}
E_s = \frac{\text{fraction of attribution mass inside } s}
           {\text{fraction of time occupied by } s},
\end{equation}
so that $E_s = 1$ is exactly what uniform temporal attention produces, $E_s > 1$
is a genuine preference and $E_s < 1$ is avoidance. The normalisation is the
whole point: a statement such as ``\SI{25}{\percent} of the attention fell in
systole'' is uninterpretable until one knows that systole occupies
\SI{23}{\percent} of the window. Because most clinically important adult murmurs
are systolic, a model that has learned pathology should show
$E_\text{systole} > 1$ --- a prediction made before the measurement.

The prediction is confirmed in sign and is very weak in magnitude.
Table~\ref{tab:alignment} gives $E_\text{systole} = \num{1.056}$, above the
uniform null but by about \SI{6}{\percent}. Against a permutation null, in which
the attribution profile is shuffled in time so that its sparsity and dynamic
range are preserved while any relation to the cardiac cycle is destroyed, only
\SI{33}{\percent} of the 200 tested windows reach significance at
$\alpha = 0.05$, the mean $p$-value is \num{0.43}, and the effect size is
Cohen's $d = \num{0.131}$. Fig.~\ref{fig:alignment} shows the corresponding
distribution: shifted right of $E = 1$, but overlapping it heavily. We state
this as a tendency, not a property of the model.

Three honesty conditions attach to these numbers. First, the segmenter is a
simplified envelope-based labeller, not the reference hidden semi-Markov
model~\cite{springer2016logistic}. It is used only at evaluation time and never
during training, so a noisier segmenter adds variance and makes the test
\emph{harder} to pass; it cannot manufacture a positive result. Second,
low-confidence windows are excluded, and the honest denominator for every figure
above is the \SI{66.5}{\percent} inclusion rate (2235 of 3362 windows, mean
segmenter confidence \num{0.559}, mean estimated heart rate \SI{82}{bpm}).
Third, Grad-CAM's last convolutional feature map is only a few cells across in
each dimension; the frequency axis is too coarse to support spectral claims, so
no frequency conclusion is drawn from Grad-CAM. All frequency evidence comes
from PWP SHAP.

\subsection{The check that fails}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_gradcam_example}
\caption{Grad-CAM on a correctly classified abnormal window, with the segmenter
state track below. Maps such as this one motivate the enrichment test; on their
own they cannot support it.}
\label{fig:gradcam}
\end{figure}

If systolic enrichment reflected murmur detection, it should be stronger when
the model is right than when it is wrong. Stratifying by outcome, it is not.
Mean $E_\text{systole}$ is \num{1.085} on true positives but \num{1.115} on
false positives and \num{1.157} on false negatives ($n = 26$, so that last figure
is a tendency at best). The difference between true and false positives is
$\num{-0.030}$: the model's systolic focus is, if anything, marginally
\emph{stronger} when it raises a false alarm.

This is the clearest negative result in the project, and it constrains what the
rest of the section can claim. The attribution maps are not artifacts --- the
sanity checks establish that --- and they do lean toward systole. But that lean
does not distinguish correct from incorrect decisions, so it cannot be used as
evidence that the model has learned murmur detection. Fig.~\ref{fig:gradcam}
shows a representative correctly classified abnormal window; visual inspection
of such maps is what suggested the hypothesis, and the quantitative test is what
prevents us from concluding it.
