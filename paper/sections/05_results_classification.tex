\subsection{Aggregate performance}

Table~\ref{tab:results} gives recording-level test performance for the five
feature/model pairings, and Table~\ref{tab:baselines} the trivial baselines that
put them in perspective. All five models clearly exceed the always-normal
predictor: MAcc ranges from \num{0.820} to \num{0.866} against \num{0.500}. The
Matthews correlation coefficient, which is zero for any constant predictor, is
between \num{0.59} and \num{0.66} for the leading models. This claim is
supported without qualification.

The claim that the CNN would outperform the best classical pairing is not.
SVM--MFCC reaches MAcc \num{0.866} \SI{95}{\percent} CI $[0.834, 0.894]$ and the
CNN \num{0.865} $[0.828, 0.900]$, a difference of \num{0.0004} inside intervals
that almost entirely coincide. On MAcc the two are statistically
indistinguishable, and so, on the interval criterion, are all five models.

The confusion matrices (Fig.~\ref{fig:confusion}) show what the aggregate hides:
the models differ chiefly in where they sit on the sensitivity/specificity
trade-off. RF--PWP achieves the highest sensitivity of all (\num{0.970}) at the
cost of the lowest specificity (\num{0.670}); the CNN is the most balanced
(\num{0.889}/\num{0.842}). For a screening application, where a missed pathology
costs more than a referral, the high-sensitivity operating points are arguably
preferable, but nothing in our evaluation selects among them. The ROC curves
(Fig.~\ref{fig:roc}) add a further nuance: the CNN has the best AUC
(\num{0.961} against \num{0.922} for SVM--MFCC) without the best MAcc, meaning
its \emph{ranking} of recordings is better than its chosen operating point. A
threshold tuned on validation data captures only part of that advantage.

\subsection{Paired comparison}

\input{tables/table_significance}

MAcc intervals and paired error tests do not agree, and the disagreement is
informative rather than contradictory. Table~\ref{tab:significance} shows that
after Holm--Bonferroni correction the CNN makes significantly fewer
recording-level errors than every other model, including SVM--MFCC
($28/11$ discordant, $p = \num{1.0e-2}$), while the two MFCC classical models
are indistinguishable from each other ($p = \num{0.21}$).

These statements are compatible. McNemar tests the count of \emph{discordant
errors} at each model's fixed operating point; MAcc averages sensitivity and
specificity, which weights the minority class far more heavily. The CNN gets
more recordings right overall, but its errors are distributed such that the
sensitivity/specificity average is unchanged. Since MAcc is the metric this task
is defined by, we do not claim the CNN is the better model; we state that it is
better on total errors and equivalent on the primary metric.

The feature domain matters more than the classifier family. Both MFCC models
outrank both PWP models, and every MFCC-versus-PWP comparison that is
significant favours MFCC. The wavelet-packet representation, adopted on the
principled grounds of Section~II, did not pay off here. We note in
Section~\ref{sec:xai} that it nevertheless remains the more useful representation
for interpretation, which is a different property from accuracy.

\subsection{The aggregate metric is not uniform across sites}
\label{sec:persite}

\input{tables/table_per_site}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_per_site_macc}
\caption{Per-sub-database MAcc of the best model, with sub-database size. The
pooled figure of \num{0.866} is dominated by \texttt{training-e}, which supplies
\SI{66}{\percent} of the test recordings and on which the model is nearly
perfect.}
\label{fig:persite}
\end{figure}

Table~\ref{tab:persite} and Fig.~\ref{fig:persite} decompose the best model's performance by
acquisition sub-database, and this is the result that most changes how the
preceding numbers should be read. Performance is not uniform. MAcc is
\num{0.986} on \texttt{training-e} and \num{0.477} on \texttt{training-a}, a
spread of \num{0.509}; on \texttt{training-a}, \texttt{training-b} and
\texttt{training-d} the model is at or below the \num{0.500} of a constant
predictor. Specificity collapses to \num{0.000} on \texttt{training-a} and
\num{0.155} on \texttt{training-b}: on those sites the model labels almost
everything abnormal.

The pooled figure is therefore an average dominated by one site.
\texttt{training-e} contributes 321 of the 484 test recordings
(\SI{66}{\percent}) and has a low abnormal prevalence of \SI{8.7}{\percent}; it
is both the largest and the easiest partition. \texttt{training-a}, by contrast,
has \SI{72.1}{\percent} abnormal prevalence, and a model that answers
``abnormal'' there is right \SI{72}{\percent} of the time by accident of
prevalence.

This is exactly the behaviour Section~\ref{sec:clustering} predicted from the
unsupervised geometry, and it is what a site-prevalence shortcut looks like when
measured. We are careful about the inference: the per-site breakdown establishes
that performance does not transfer across acquisition conditions, which is a
measurement. That the mechanism is prevalence-driven site recognition is an
inference, supported by the clustering result and by the direction of the
failures but not directly demonstrated --- we did not, for example, train a site
classifier on the same features, which would be the natural confirming
experiment and is the clearest gap in this study.

The smaller sub-databases (\texttt{training-d}, $n=8$; \texttt{training-f},
$n=17$) carry very little weight, and their figures should be read as tendencies
rather than findings. \texttt{training-c} ($n=4$) contains a single class in the
test split and is omitted.

\subsection{Overfitting and calibration}

Two weaknesses are worth stating. Both random forests show a large gap between
training and cross-validated balanced accuracy --- \num{0.947} versus
\num{0.867} on MFCC, and \num{0.945} versus \num{0.814} on PWP --- so they are
memorising the training folds despite the depth and leaf-size grid; stronger
regularisation would be the obvious next step. Second, the classical models are
poorly calibrated, with expected calibration error \num{0.129} for RF--MFCC and
\num{0.150} for RF--PWP. Since recording-level decisions are formed by averaging
window probabilities, miscalibration propagates into the aggregation, and the
close agreement between mean-probability and majority-vote aggregation
(within \num{0.004} MAcc) suggests the probabilities are contributing little
beyond their ordering.

\subsection{Comparison with published work}

\input{tables/table_literature}

Table~\ref{tab:literature} places our best result alongside published CinC 2016
entries. Our MAcc of \num{0.866} sits at the top of the \num{0.84}--\num{0.86}
band those entries occupy. This must not be read as an improvement. The published
figures were obtained on the official hidden test set, which was never released;
ours come from a self-defined split of the public training data, with a
different class balance, a different site mix and a different difficulty. The
numbers are of the same order, which is the only conclusion the comparison
supports, and it is reassuring mainly as evidence that the pipeline is not
broken.
